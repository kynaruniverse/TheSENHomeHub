EHCP & Legal Guides

Plain-English guides to EHCPs, your legal rights, and home educating a child with SEN in England & Wales

⚠️ Please note This is a general information guide only — not legal advice. Laws and processes may change. Always check GOV.UK for the most up-to-date official information, and consider contacting IPSEA or SOS!SEN for personalised support.

What is an EHCP?

An Education, Health and Care Plan (EHCP) is a legal document for children and young people aged 0–25 in England who have special educational needs and/or disabilities (SEND) that cannot be met by the support usually available in a mainstream school or setting.

What does an EHCP cover?

The EHCP has sections A–K. The key ones are:

  • Section B — Your child's special educational needs in detail
  • Section E — The outcomes you want your child to achieve
  • Section F — The specific educational provision that must be made (this is legally binding)
  • Section I — The name and type of educational setting
  • Section H — Social care provision

💡 The most important thing to know

Section F of an EHCP is legally binding. Whatever provision is named in Section F must be provided. If it isn't, you can challenge this. This is why getting Section F wording right is so important.

Who can have an EHCP?

A child may be eligible if they:

  • Have a disability or condition that affects their learning
  • Need more support than is ordinarily available at their school
  • Are aged 0–25 (EHCPs can continue into further education and training)
  • Live in England (Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have different systems)

⚠️ Wales, Scotland & Northern Ireland

EHCPs apply in England only. Wales uses Individual Development Plans (IDPs). Scotland uses Co-ordinated Support Plans (CSPs). Northern Ireland uses Statements of Special Educational Needs. The principles are similar but the processes differ.

How to get an EHCP

The process of getting an EHCP is called an EHC needs assessment. You, a school, or another professional can request this. The whole process has legal time limits the local authority must follow.

The EHCP process — step by step

1

Request an EHC needs assessment

You (as a parent), the school, or another professional can request the local authority (LA) carries out an EHC needs assessment. Write to the SEND team at your local council. State clearly why you think your child needs an EHCP.

Tip: Keep a copy of everything you send and note the date. The LA must respond within 6 weeks.
2

LA decides whether to assess

The LA has 6 weeks to decide whether to carry out a full assessment. They will inform you of their decision. If they refuse, you have the right to appeal to the SEND Tribunal.

Tip: Gather evidence before requesting — school reports, diagnosis letters, GP or CAMHS letters, therapy reports. The more evidence, the stronger the request.
3

The assessment takes place

The LA will gather information from you, the school, and professionals (educational psychologist, speech therapist, OT, etc.). You will be asked to contribute. This is your chance to describe your child's needs in detail — don't hold back.

4

Draft EHCP issued

If the LA decides to issue an EHCP, they must send you a draft within 16 weeks of the initial request. You have 15 days to comment and request changes. You can name a school or educational setting at this stage.

Tip: Read every word of the draft carefully — especially Section F. Make sure the provision is specific and measurable, not vague (e.g. "daily 20-minute 1:1 phonics sessions" not just "phonics support").
5

Final EHCP issued

The final EHCP must be issued within 20 weeks of the original request. If you disagree with the final plan, you can appeal to the SEND Tribunal within 2 months.

Key timelines

Week 0

You submit the request

Send a written request to your LA's SEND team.

By Week 6

LA decides whether to assess

They must tell you yes or no within 6 weeks.

By Week 16

Draft EHCP issued (if going ahead)

You have 15 days to comment on the draft.

By Week 20

Final EHCP issued

The complete, legally binding plan is issued.

Home Education & the Law

In England, parents have the legal right to educate their children at home. You do not need to be a qualified teacher. However, the rules change if your child has an EHCP.

✅ The basic legal position

  • Under the Education Act 1996, parents must ensure their child receives a "suitable, full-time education" — but this does not have to be at school.
  • You do not need LA permission to home educate (with one important exception — see below).
  • You do not need to follow the National Curriculum.
  • There is no requirement to have set hours, terms or holidays.
  • You do not need to have your child assessed or inspected (though the LA can make enquiries).

⚠️ The EHCP exception — very important

If your child has an EHCP and is currently named in a school in Section I, you must get the LA's consent before withdrawing them to home educate. The LA must agree to amend the EHCP to name "home education" in Section I.

If your child does not currently have a school named (e.g. they have never attended school), you do not need consent — but the LA still has a duty to ensure provision in the EHCP is being met.

📝 What "suitable education" means

The education must be suitable to your child's age, ability, aptitude, and any special educational needs. It does not have to look like school — it can be child-led, project-based, or structured around your child's specific needs and strengths.

LA involvement in home education

📬

Notifying the LA

If you are withdrawing your child from a state school, you must notify the school in writing. The school will then inform the LA. You do not need to register with the LA yourself, but many parents choose to make contact proactively.

🔍

LA monitoring

The LA has a duty to identify children not receiving a suitable education. They may contact you to ask for information about your home education provision. You are not obliged to allow home visits, but providing written information about your approach is reasonable.

Tip: Keep a simple learning journal or portfolio. Photos, samples of work, and notes on activities are all useful if the LA makes enquiries.
📋

When you have an EHCP

The LA retains its duty to ensure EHCP provision is met even when you home educate. This means they should still arrange or fund any provision named in Section F. In practice, many families have to advocate strongly for this — organisations like IPSEA can help.

Your Rights as a Parent

Knowing your rights means you can advocate effectively for your child. Here is a plain-English summary of the key rights parents and carers have under SEND law in England.

Your rightWhat it means in practice
Right to request an EHC needs assessmentAny parent can request an assessment at any time. The LA must consider it.
Right to be involved in the processYou must be consulted at every stage. Your views must be included in the EHCP.
Right to name a school or settingYou can request a specific school (including independent or specialist schools) in Section I.
Right to appealYou can appeal to the SEND Tribunal if the LA refuses to assess, refuses to issue an EHCP, or if you disagree with the content or named setting.
Right to an annual reviewThe EHCP must be reviewed at least once a year. You can request an early review at any time.
Right to mediationBefore going to tribunal, you must be offered mediation (though you don't have to use it).
Right to home educateYou can choose to home educate (with LA consent if a school is named in the EHCP).
Right to informationThe LA must publish a Local Offer describing all available SEND support in the area.

🏛️ SEND Tribunal appeals

The First-tier Tribunal (SEND) hears appeals from parents in England. You can appeal against: refusal to assess; refusal to issue an EHCP; the content of Sections B, F, or I; refusal to maintain an EHCP; and the named educational setting. You generally have 2 months from the LA's decision to appeal. Free support is available from IPSEA and SOS!SEN.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the questions we hear most often from home-educating SEN families. Tap any question to expand the answer.

Can I home educate my child if they have an EHCP? +
Yes — but if a school is named in Section I of the EHCP, you must get the LA's agreement before withdrawing your child. Write to the LA requesting they amend the EHCP to name "home education." They cannot unreasonably withhold consent, and the duty to fund provision in Section F remains.
Does the LA still have to fund provision if I home educate? +
Yes — if there is an EHCP with provision named in Section F, the LA retains the duty to ensure that provision is made, even during home education. In practice, this is not always straightforward and many families find they have to actively pursue this. IPSEA can advise on specific cases.
What if the LA refuses to issue an EHCP? +
You can appeal to the First-tier Tribunal (SEND) within 2 months of the decision. Before doing so, you must be offered mediation (though you can decline). IPSEA and SOS!SEN offer free advice and support for tribunal cases and have a strong track record of helping families succeed.
Do I need to follow the National Curriculum when home educating? +
No. Home-educating parents in England are not required to follow the National Curriculum. The legal requirement is to provide a "suitable, full-time education" appropriate to your child's age, ability, aptitude, and SEN. This gives you significant flexibility to tailor learning to your child's needs.
Can the LA force me to let them visit my home? +
No — you are not legally required to allow LA officials into your home to inspect your education provision. However, you can be asked to provide information demonstrating that your child is receiving a suitable education. Sharing a written summary or learning portfolio is a reasonable response to LA enquiries.
What is the Local Offer and how do I find mine? +
Every LA in England must publish a Local Offer — a directory of all SEND services, support, and provision available in their area. Search "[your council name] Local Offer" to find yours. It should cover education, health, and social care services for children and young people aged 0–25 with SEND.
My child has a diagnosis but no EHCP — can they still get support? +
Yes. A diagnosis does not automatically lead to an EHCP, and an EHCP is not the only form of support available. Schools have a duty to make reasonable adjustments under the Equality Act 2010, and SEN support (previously called School Action and School Action Plus) should be available without an EHCP. However, if your child's needs are significant, it is worth requesting an EHCP assessment.
What age does an EHCP stop? +
An EHCP can be maintained until age 25 if the young person is in education or training. It does not automatically end at 16 or 18. However, the EHCP must be reviewed annually, and the LA may cease it if they decide it is no longer needed — which you can appeal.
